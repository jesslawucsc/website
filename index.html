How do I became a linguist?

I'm a plogyglot with a good command of Hakka, Mandarin, Cantonese, and English. I also have some command of Spanish and Japanese. Below is how I met these languages and engaged with them over the years.

Hakka: I spent my early childhood living on a Hakka village in Guangdong, China. In the 80s, villages had few immigrants, so I lived in a largely monolingual community. When I traveled to nearby villages, I would hear slightly different Hakka varieties and puzzle over these differences.

Mandarin: My family spent a few years living in a city for better career and education prospects. I rapidly acquired Southern Mandarin between 7 and 10. After my family moved to Hong Kong, my Mandarin acquisition was split into a P-route (phonetics and phonology) and a S-route (syntax and semantics). I learned Mandarin phonetics (mainly pronunciation) in a foreign language class I had once a week. (Mandarin was considered a foreign language in Hong Kong in the 90s.) I learned Mandarin syntax and semantics (with Cantonese pronunciation) in regular Chinese classes, primarily from reading and writing. I spoke Hong Kong Mandarin (港普) until I started dating a Northern Mandarin speaker. My proficiency improved greatly after that.

Cantonese: I acquired Cantonese (with Traditional Chinese Writing) rapidly after my family moved to Hong Kong in the 90s. Cantonese was considered a dialect and not a proper language for education at that time by the general public. I never learned Cantonese grammar until I majored in linguistics. Supported by the world-class teaching and research at CUHK, I learned a lot about Cantonese grammar. For example, I participated in and learned that my Cantonese only had 4 contrastive tones, instead of the standard 6.

English: English acquisitin happened quite late for me, and it progressed very slowly. I struggled with this language greatly and always thought that its grammar couldn't be more different from Chinese languages. My view started to change as I learned more about Noam Chomsky's hypothesis that all languages share a universal grammar. I started to see similarities and differences in English and Chinese languages in light of this hypothesis. Spanish: I studied Spanish in college and lived in Spanish for two months. Although I no longer have good command of Spanish, my limited proficiency in it has always helped me make friends around the world.

Japanese: I studied Japanese in college. One of my professors, Kazutoshi Ono (大野和敏), is an excellent linguist. His teaching was unparalleled, and helped me see that linguistics could bring language teaching to the next level. I would later model my Chinese grammar course after his Japanese grammar course.
